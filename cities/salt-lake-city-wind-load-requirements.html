Salt Lake City/Salt Lake County: Wasatch Mountain Valley Wind Load Requirements

Salt Lake City, located in Salt Lake County, Utah, sits in a valley between the Wasatch Mountains to the east and the Oquirrh Mountains to the west at approximately 4,200 feet elevation. Salt Lake City requires a design wind speed of approximately 90-105 mph (3-second gust) for Risk Category II structures, based on ASCE 7-22 wind speed maps and its mountain valley location.

Salt Lake City experiences unique wind load considerations due to its position in the Salt Lake Valley, with potential topographic effects from the Wasatch Range creating enhanced wind conditions in canyon mouths and elevated areas. The city has grown from its Mormon pioneer heritage into a major tech hub known as Silicon Slopes, hosting the 2002 Winter Olympics. The city operates under the Utah State Construction Code which adopts the International Building Code (IBC) with state-specific amendments.

Why Salt Lake City Has 90-105 mph Wind Speed Requirements

Salt Lake City's design wind speeds of 90-105 mph are derived from ASCE 7-22 wind speed maps for the northern Utah valley region. This velocity range reflects the city's interior mountain valley location, elevation at approximately 4,200 feet, and exposure to mountain wind phenomena including canyon winds and downslope wind events.

The exact wind speed for a specific Salt Lake City project depends on precise location and elevation. Areas near the mouths of mountain canyons (Big Cottonwood Canyon, Little Cottonwood Canyon, Parley's Canyon) may experience enhanced wind speeds due to canyon channeling effects. The Wasatch Front foothills and elevated areas typically require higher wind speeds than the valley floor. Wasatch Mountain Topographic Effects

Salt Lake City's position at the base of the Wasatch Range creates unique topographic wind considerations:

  • Canyon Winds: Mountain canyons (Big Cottonwood, Little Cottonwood, Parley's, Emigration) can channel and accelerate winds, requiring topographic factor Kzt greater than 1.0 for projects near canyon mouths
  • Downslope Winds: Cold air drainage from the mountains can create strong downslope wind events during winter months
  • Wasatch Front Development: Projects on the mountain bench areas face greater wind exposure than valley floor locations
  • Lake Effect: Great Salt Lake to the west can influence local wind patterns, particularly in western valley areas
  • Elevation Gradients: Wind speeds increase with elevation, requiring adjustment for projects at higher elevations along the Wasatch Front

Salt Lake City Winter and Mountain Climate

Salt Lake City's mountain valley climate creates specific engineering considerations:

  • Winter Inversion: Temperature inversions can trap cold air in the valley, creating unique atmospheric conditions
  • Snow Loads: Significant snow loads must be considered in addition to wind loads, particularly at higher elevations
  • Seasonal Wind Patterns: Winter and spring typically experience stronger wind events than summer months
  • Lake Effect Weather: Great Salt Lake influences local weather patterns and wind conditions
  • Mountain Storms: Spring and fall mountain storms can bring high winds to canyon areas and the Wasatch Front

Salt Lake City Wind Load Calculations: Step by Step

Calculating wind loads for Salt Lake City projects requires following ASCE 7-22 methodology with Utah State Construction Code requirements. The fundamental velocity pressure equation is:

qz = 0.00256 Kz Kzt Kd Ke V¬≤

For a typical Salt Lake City valley project with V = 95 mph and Exposure B conditions, a downtown project with:

  • V = 95 mph (Salt Lake City valley velocity)
  • Kz = 0.70 (15 ft height, Exposure B)
  • Kzt = 1.0 (valley floor, no significant topographic effects)
  • Kd = 0.85 (buildings)
  • Ke = 1.0 (elevation adjustment typically incorporated into base wind speed)

Results in a velocity pressure of approximately qz = 16.4 psf. However, a project near a canyon mouth or on the Wasatch Front may require Kzt = 1.2 or higher, significantly increasing pressures.

Exposure Categories: Valley Floor vs. Mountain Slopes

Salt Lake City projects require careful exposure category determination based on location and surrounding terrain:

  • Exposure Category B (Urban Valley): Most common for Salt Lake City proper with urban development, tree coverage, and building density. Downtown and established residential neighborhoods typically qualify for Exposure B
  • Exposure Category C (Open Areas): May apply to areas near Great Salt Lake, open agricultural land in the valley, or exposed foothill locations with limited obstruction
  • Exposure Category D (Rare): Generally not applicable in Salt Lake City unless project is in completely open terrain with extensive unobstructed exposure
  • Transitional Zones: Projects on the urban-rural fringe or in developing areas may require careful exposure analysis

Professional Engineer (PE) Requirements in Salt Lake City

All wind load calculations for Salt Lake City building permits must be prepared by or under the direct supervision of a Professional Engineer (PE) licensed in Utah. The sealed calculations must include:

  • Project address and zip code with wind speed determination
  • Design wind speed (90-105 mph range for Risk Category II)
  • Exposure Category determination with supporting justification
  • Topographic factor (Kzt) analysis, especially for projects near canyon mouths or mountain slopes
  • Risk Category determination with supporting documentation
  • Complete ASCE 7-22 calculation methodology
  • Component and Cladding (C&C) pressures for all building elements
  • Main Wind Force Resisting System (MWFRS) pressures if applicable
  • Combined wind and snow load analysis for roof design

Utah requires professional engineers to be licensed through the Division of Occupational and Professional Licensing (DOPL). Engineers must hold a valid Utah PE license to seal structural calculations for projects in Salt Lake City and throughout Utah.

Common Salt Lake City Wind Load Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring topographic effects: Projects near canyon mouths or on mountain slopes require Kzt greater than 1.0, significantly increasing wind pressures
  • Using wrong wind speed: Salt Lake City requires 90-105 mph depending on location and elevation, verify using ASCE 7-22 maps
  • Incorrect exposure category: Most urban Salt Lake City qualifies for Exposure B, but exposed foothill areas may require Exposure C
  • Neglecting elevation effects: Projects at higher elevations along the Wasatch Front require higher wind speeds than valley floor
  • Forgetting canyon wind channeling: Big Cottonwood, Little Cottonwood, and Parley's Canyon create enhanced wind conditions at canyon mouths
  • Not considering snow and wind combination: Both loads must be properly combined per ASCE 7-22 for roof design
  • Incorrect Risk Category: Schools, hospitals, and assembly buildings require higher categories and wind speeds
  • Outdated calculations: Must use ASCE 7-22 per current Utah State Construction Code
