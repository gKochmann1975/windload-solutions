What is an Escarpment?

An escarpment is a cliff or steep slope separating two relatively level areas of different elevations. Escarpments cause wind to accelerate as it flows up and over the sharp edge, creating a speed-up zone on the upper level near the crest.

Common examples include:

  • Coastal bluffs overlooking the ocean
  • River valley walls
  • Plateau edges (mesas)
  • Man-made cuts (highway embankments, quarry walls)
  • Fault scarps

K2 - Horizontal Distance Factor

  • K2 = 1.0 at the crest
  • Decreases with distance upwind and downwind from crest
  • K2 = 0 beyond the speed-up zone

K3 - Height Above Ground Factor

  • K3 = 1.0 at ground level
  • Decreases linearly with height above ground
  • K3 accounts for reduction in topographic effects with elevation above local terrain

Design Considerations for Escarpment Sites

  • Greatest effects at crest: Buildings located right at the escarpment edge experience maximum wind pressure increases
  • Wind direction matters: Speed-up only occurs for winds perpendicular to the escarpment edge
  • Lower level unaffected: Structures below the escarpment typically have Kzt = 1.0
  • Setback considerations: Moving structures back from the edge reduces Kzt effects
