What is Flat Terrain?

Flat terrain refers to sites with no significant topographic features that would cause wind speed-up effects. The vast majority of development sites fall into this category. When a site is classified as flat terrain, the topographic factor Kzt = 1.0, meaning there is no amplification of wind speeds due to terrain effects.

For Flat Terrain:
Kzt = 1.0

This is the default and most common condition. Wind load calculations use the base wind speed from ASCE 7 wind speed maps without any topographic adjustments.

When is a Site Considered Flat Terrain?

A site is flat terrain when ANY of these conditions exist:

  • No significant elevation changes: The surrounding area within 2 miles upwind is relatively level
  • Upwind slope less than 10%: If there are elevation changes, the upwind slope (H/Lh) is less than 0.10
  • Not isolated: Topographic features are not isolated (surrounded by similar features at similar elevations)
  • Below minimum height criteria: Hills or ridges don't meet ASCE 7's minimum height requirements
  • Outside speed-up zones: Site is located far from any hills, ridges, or escarpments (beyond the defined speed-up zone distances)
üìç Key Point: If you're unsure whether your site has topographic effects, start by assuming Kzt = 1.0. Only calculate Kzt if you have clear evidence of an isolated hill, ridge, or escarpment meeting all ASCE 7 criteria.

Examples of Flat Terrain Sites

  • Urban and suburban developments: Most residential and commercial sites in developed areas
  • Agricultural land: Farmland, prairies, plains with minimal elevation change
  • Coastal areas: Beach developments away from dunes or bluffs
  • River valleys: Development on valley floors (not on valley sides)
  • Plateau surfaces: Development on top of plateaus (not near edges)

Why Kzt = 1.0 for Flat Terrain

Wind speed-up occurs when airflow is compressed or accelerated over topographic features. On flat terrain, there is no compression of airflow, no acceleration around obstacles, and no orographic effects. The wind speed remains consistent with the regional wind climate represented in ASCE 7 wind speed maps.

Wind Behavior Over Flat Terrain:

  • Wind flows horizontally without vertical deflection
  • No channeling or acceleration effects
  • Wind speed determined primarily by exposure category (B, C, or D)
  • Surface roughness is the dominant factor, not topography

ASCE 7 Requirements for Flat Terrain

Per ASCE 7-22 Section 26.8, when a site does not meet the conditions for topographic speed-up effects, Kzt shall be taken as 1.0. This includes:

  • Sites where the topographic feature height does not meet minimum criteria
üí° Design Simplification: Using Kzt = 1.0 simplifies wind load calculations and is conservative for most flat sites. You do not need to provide justification for Kzt = 1.0 unless the building official questions topographic conditions.

Documentation for Flat Terrain Sites

While detailed topographic analysis is not required for flat terrain, it's good practice to document:

  • Site photos: Show general terrain conditions
  • USGS topographic map: Demonstrate no significant elevation changes
  • Google Earth profile: Show terrain elevation along cardinal directions
  • Statement in calculations: "Site is located on flat terrain with no isolated topographic features. Kzt = 1.0 per ASCE 7-22 Section 26.8."
